An infectious side to Alzheimer’s disease

Potential Alzheimer's treatments have all ended up in the disease's infamous dream graveyard. Despite greatest attempts, medications targeting two proteins that accumulate in Alzheimer's disease—beta-amyloid and mutant tau—have persistently failed in human treatment trials.

Scientists are starting to think outside the box this year, and fresh theories about how the disease is started and progresses are gaining traction.

Several research published in October provided some of the best evidence yet that herpes simplex virus type I (HSV1), the irritating virus responsible for cold sores, could be a possible trigger. Since the 1990s, scientists have known that HSV1 increases the risk of Alzheimer's disease in persons who have a specific version of a gene called APOE4.

The majority of people are infected with HSV1 as children, and the virus then goes dormant until extrinsic triggers such as stress reawaken it. According to the new notion, recurrent activation of the virus in the brain during maturity could produce cumulative damage, particularly in the elderly with compromised immune function. If the notion is correct, anti-viral medications could represent a new treatment option.

Furthermore, beta-amyloid may be contagious. A British researchers injected cadaver-derived growth hormones (eww) synthesized in the 1980s into mice and discovered large beta-amyloid clumps in their brains. While this does not imply that Alzheimer's disease is contagious, it does raise the possibility that medical treatments such as brain surgery may offer a risk for transporting harmful forms of the protein from one brain to another.
