A Streetcar Named Desire

INTRODUCTION

A fading southern belle, Blanche DuBois is losing her grip on reality. As the play progresses, Blanche becomes increasingly detached. Having lost her family fortune, she suffers from an alcohol problem and a damaged reputation. Her alcohol problem can be interpreted as a coping mechanism for the stresses in her life, some of which are self-induced. Blanche has a snobbish public persona that is her way of hiding her financial situation and tarnished reputation. Privately, she is insecure and vulnerable. This contradictory nature is explored through the use of multiple literary devices, some of which are explored at a greater length below. This essay will thus explore the creativity with which Tennessee Williams uses dramatic and linguistic techniques to portray the contradictory identity of Blanche in the play ‘A Streetcar Named Desire’. The essay will be divided into three parts, each representing a separate device. It aims to explore the use of these devices and their subsequent influence on Blanche’s contradictory nature.

NARRATIVE

In the opening scene of the play, the audience is introduced to Blanche, a ‘daintily dressed’ woman who carried a ‘delicate beauty’. As she sneaks a drink, her alcoholism is revealed to the audience. After helping herself to a tumbler of whiskey that she has qualms about, Tennessee Williams shows the audience at the very onset that she is heavily driven by desire. At this point in the play, it is evident that Blanche is a social pariah. She is a widow who has lost her family fortune and estate. As the rising action of the play unfurls, the multiple strata of Blanche’s character are revealed.

During a game of poker at their house, the audience is introduced to Stanley’s friend Mitch, who becomes a potential love interest to Blanche. During their first interaction alone, Blanche selectively opens up about her past and reveals her trauma to Mitch. She is voluntarily vulnerable for the first time and it is clear at this instance that Blanche uses flirtation as a coping mechanism as she believes that in order to live a stable and secure life again, she needs a man. After having overheard a conversation between Stella and Blanche where Blanche tries to talk stella into leaving Stanley, Stanley is angered and tells the details of Blanche’s sordid past to Mitch. The details of her affair with a student are revealed to the audience. At this instance, her self-destructive tendencies are revealed to the audience. During her final visit with Mitch, these tendencies catch up to her. It is because of her past indulgences that Mitch deems her an unfit partner.

In the climax of the play, Blanche is raped by Stanley. At this instance, Blanche’s character is revealed for who she is; a helpless, fading southern belle who heavily relies on men for stability and validation. Towards the resolution, Blanche is sent to a mental hospital, taken away by a doctor while she patiently waited for her date to arrive.

IMAGERY

Throughout the play, Tennessee Williams uses imagery to depict conflicts, motives and themes. Light and dark imagery is used extensively to portray Blanche’s character and the assimilation of her many layers. Auditory imagery is used to emphasize significant events in the play. This section of the essay will explore the use of imagery in the portrayal of Blanche’s contradictory identities and their assimilation with each other.

Light and dark imagery:

At the very onset of the play, an exchange between Blanche and Stella reveals Blanche’s contempt for harsh light. Following this, blanche is rarely seen in severe lighting. Her disdain for it stems from her desire to conceal her true self and age to potential suitors. She only prefers to see Mitch in the dark or after dusk. She does so as her fantasy of being a beautiful young debutante makes her want to hide her fading beauty from herself and others. when the reality of her past is exposed, mitch insists on seeing her in direct light. The use of direct light emphasizes Blanche’s vulnerability in the moment. She is completely out of control and helpless. Figuratively, the Blanche known to the audience under dim lights is vastly contradictory to her own personality; hence the use of harsh lights when she is exposed.

Auditory imagery:

Tennessee Williams distinctively uses auditory imagery in combination with important events that highlight Blanche’s persona. The many musical motifs have their own roles and purposes in the play. The Varsuviana polka for example was the music playing moments before Blanche’s husband killed himself. She often hears the polka at instances when she feels sorry about his death. The song ‘It’s Only a Paper Moon’ shows her descent into insanity. The blue piano represents sadness and is often heard during mournful moments in the play, for example when blanche breaks the news of the loss of Belle Reve to Stella. Its music intensifies until it is overshadowed by the sound of a passing trail as Stanley is about rape Blanche, where she seems completely helpless and sheds her snobbish public persona.

STAGE DIRECTION

Tennessee Williams is often acknowledged for the elaborate stage directions he includes in his plays, often adding to their already refined details. The stage directions in a streetcar named desire effect all aspects of the story, especially its characterisation. It often symbolises a character’s emotions and shows the physical and emotional perspectives of the characters.

Blanche DuBois’s mental health problems are displayed in part by her actions but to the audience, it is most discernible by the tone of her voice and her motions. There is an absolute lack of privacy in the house, often making Blanche feel overwhelmed by the presence of people around her, especially Stanley. In the opening scene, blanche is described as ‘daintily dressed’ and having a ‘delicate beauty’. To the audience, Blanche seems like a tasteful and refined woman. Here, the audience are introduced to her persona. Towards the end of the play however, stage directions depict Blanche as an unstable, delusional woman as she ‘murmurs excitedly as if to a group of spectral admirers’ and describe her turning around ‘weakly’ and hesitantly’. Towards the end of the play, the audience is well aware of both; Blanche’s mental condition as well as her façade. the identity that she so carefully tries to hide is a reality everyone can see, especially after Stanley rapes her.

CONCLUSION

In conclusion, Tennessee Williams uses multiple literary devices, each in their own unique ways to depict the duality within Blanche’s character as the play progresses. While the plot progression slowly reveals Blanche’s true nature, imagery and stage direction depict her mental state at one instance. Upon studying the progression in imagery and stage direction, Blanche’s loosening grip on reality is revealed. The themes of the play are relevant to the time period as America saw a shift from the estate holders and ‘old money’ to an increase in the working class. In a more modern context however, the play talks about rape, homosexuality, mental health, domestic violence, and many other issues that are extremely relevant to movements and social causes that are heavily talked about today.
