SUPERFICIALLY USELESS BODY PARTS

When I say body parts that are superficially useless, I mean that not many of us know what they are actually used for. So let's go over a few of them below.

Number 1 : The dangly thing in the back of your throat

This is formally called a Palatine Uvula (not to be confused with the uvula of urinary bladder), commonly referred to as simply the Uvula. During swallowing, the soft palate and the uvula move together to close off the nasopharynx (part of throat behind mouth and nasal cavity), and hence prevent food from entering the nasal cavity. It produces an abundant amount of saliva which keeps the throat lubricated. The uvula can also contribute to snoring or heavy breathing during sleep. Having an elongated uvula can cause vibrations that lead to snoring. In some cases, this leads to sleep apnea, which may be treated by removal of the uvula or part of it if necessary, with an operation known as uvulopalatopharyngoplasty.

Number 2 : The arch - the part below your foot that doesn't touch the ground

The arch below the inner part of your foot is designed in a similar manner to a spring: bears the weight of the body and absorbs the shock that is produced with locomotion. The foot's flexibility conferred by the arches is what facilitates everyday loco-motor functions such as walking and sprinting.

Number 3 : Men’s nipples

Men’s nipples are actually useless but their origin is quite interesting. Everybody starts off as a woman in the womb. For 60 days, all embryos follow the same genetic activity, until testosterone changes the course of action for those with a Y chromosome. Men’s nipples are the remaining, non-functional product of those first two months.

Number 4 : Skin

Did you know that we have glow-in-the-dark skin ?!

Japanese scientists used ultra-sensitive cameras to discover humans actually give off light, sort of like a firefly. You glow the most from your forehead, cheeks, and neck. The glow is the faintest when it’s late at night and at its brightest in late afternoon. Sadly, this ‘bioluminescence’ is too faint for the human eye to see.
