TODAY’S LANDFILLS, TOMORROW’S MINES

Landfill mining involves the excavation and processing of waste from closed landfill sites or the completed sections of operational landfill sites. It is not a new concept. The first recorded example occurred in 1953 at the Hiriya landfill near Tel Aviv, Israel.

At present, commercial prospects for landfill mining are theoretical. With the passage of time, it starts to seem necessary. The typical process of landfill mining involves excavating the contents of a landfill cell, separating bulky materials, sorting the excavated material into manageable stockpiles and then separating soil (used as a cover material during landfilling) from waste using a revolving cylinder or vibrating screens. Further separation of ferrous materials can then be completed using a magnetic separator.

Certain materials that were discarded in the past have now found various uses and these materials can be found in landfills. Discarded electronics can contain gold, platinum and other valuable metals. Waste found in landfills can also be used to generate energy (electricity and heat). Countries such as Norway and Sweden are ahead of the world in this field; they even import waste from other countries to generate energy. Landfill mining can increase void landfill space which is becoming scarce as the years pass by.

Apart from these, there are some very surprising things one can find in a landfill such as designer bags and clothes. Fashion brands such as Burberry and Louis Vuitton burn or throw away their unsold products in order to maintain their value; in order for them to remain rare and accessible only to few. Landfills can also have explosives, cars, and so much more.

The concept of landfill mining merits serious consideration. The rest of the world should follow in Norway and Sweden’s footsteps in the waste to energy initiative and the world will be better for it.
