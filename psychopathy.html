PSYCHOPATHY

Chances are that you have used the word ‘Psychopath’ at least once in your life. By definition, a psychopath is a person suffering from a chronic mental disorder with abnormal or violent social behavior.

Psychopathy can potentially be caused by genetic and non-genetic factors. Genetic factors may generally influence the development of psychopathy while environmental factors affect the specific expression of the traits that predominate. There is a wide range of environmental factors that can contribute to psychopathy such as having a convicted parent, being physically or emotionally neglected, having low social class, etc. One type of psychopathy called ‘Acquired Psychopathy’ can be caused by traumatic brain injury, possibly damaging the Prefrontal Cortex or the Amygdala (located within the temporal lobes of the brain). With the help of MRI studies, experts have visualized brain abnormalities and differences of individuals with psychopathy in areas of the brain that control emotions, social interactions, ethics, morality, regret, impulsivity and conscience. High levels of testosterone combined with low levels of cortisol and/or serotonin can also be contributing factors.

Another study showed that both adults and juveniles high in psychopathic traits exhibited dramatically decreased activity in the paralimbic regions, primarily the anterior and posterior cingulate. This gives a central paradox to the definition of a psychopath : a completely rational but morally insane person. The psychopath is missing the moral core, a core that appears intimately involved with the paralimbic regions. So when a psychopath gives a moral response, it is postulated that he recruits frontal areas to mimic his dysfunctional paralimbic areas.

Research on Psychopathy has largely been done on men while a condition called Hysteria was largely partial towards women. Globally, an average of 17.5% of the prisoners are believed to be psychopaths. Many criminals use the plea of having a ‘psychopathic disorder’ to avoid incarceration. Historically, most clinicians and academics have believed that psychopathy is untreatable. Many approaches such as psychoanalysis, group therapy, client-centered therapy, psychodrama, psychosurgery, electroshock therapy and drug therapy seem to have failed. To the best of our knowledge, no pill can instill empathy, no vaccine can prevent violent behavior and no amount of therapy can change an uncaring mind. But this has not stopped scientists who study psychopaths from trying to bring them back.
